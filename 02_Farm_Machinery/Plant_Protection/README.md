## üéØ Introduction

### What is Plant Protection?

Plant protection involves controlling pests, diseases, and weeds to ensure healthy crop growth and maximum yield. Plant protection equipment is used to apply pesticides, herbicides, fungicides, and other chemicals efficiently.

### Types of Application

**1. Spraying:**
- Liquid chemicals applied as droplets
- Most common method
- Size: 10-600 microns

**2. Dusting:**
- Dry powder application
- Less common
- Particle size: 1-50 microns

**3. Fumigation:**
- Gaseous chemicals
- Storage pest control

---

### Importance of Plant Protection Equipment

**Benefits:**
- ‚úÖ Uniform chemical distribution
- ‚úÖ Precise application rate
- ‚úÖ Labor saving
- ‚úÖ Reduced chemical wastage
- ‚úÖ Timely pest control
- ‚úÖ Better crop yield

**Challenges:**
- ‚ö† Drift issues
- ‚ö† Operator safety
- ‚ö† Environmental concerns
- ‚ö† Calibration requirements

### Knapsack Sprayer (Detailed)

#### Components

**1. Tank:**
- Capacity: 10-20 liters
- Material: Plastic/Metal
- Rectangular or cylindrical
- Filler opening with strainer

**2. Pump:**
- Piston or diaphragm type
- Creates pressure: 2-4 bar
- Lever operated

**3. Pressure Chamber:**
- Stores compressed air
- Maintains constant pressure
- Smooth spray flow

**4. Delivery Hose:**
- Flexible rubber/PVC
- Length: 1-1.5 m
- Connects tank to lance

**5. Lance (Wand):**
- Hollow tube
- Length: 50-75 cm
- Directs spray

**6. Nozzle:**
- At lance end
- Creates spray pattern
- Determines droplet size

**7. Cut-off Valve:**
- On/off control
- On lance handle

**8. Pressure Gauge:**
- Indicates operating pressure
- Ensures correct application

**9. Shoulder Straps:**
- Padded for comfort
- Adjustable

---

#### Working Principle

**Step 1:** Fill tank with spray solution (chemical + water)

**Step 2:** Close tank lid

**Step 3:** Pump air into tank using lever
- Creates pressure: 2-4 bar
- Air compresses above liquid

**Step 4:** Open cut-off valve
- Pressure forces liquid through hose
- Reaches nozzle
- Atomizes into droplets

**Step 5:** Spray application
- Periodic pumping to maintain pressure
- Even coverage of crop

---

#### Types of Knapsack Sprayers

**A. Lever-Operated (Hand Compression):**
- Pump before spraying
- Pre-compression type
- Pressure drops gradually
- Need periodic pumping

**B. Continuous Pressure (Pneumatic):**
- Lever on side
- Continuous pumping while spraying
- Constant pressure
- More uniform spray

**C. Motorized Knapsack:**
- Small petrol engine (1-2 HP)
- Mounted on back
- High pressure (8-15 bar)
- Battery ignition
- Continuous operation

### Boom Sprayer (Detailed)

#### Description

A boom sprayer has a long horizontal boom with multiple nozzles mounted at regular intervals. It's typically tractor-mounted for field crops.

**2. Pump:**

**3. Boom:**
- Length: 6-24 m

**4. Nozzles:**
- Number: 12-48

**5. Control System:**
- Pressure gauge
- Flow meter
- Section control valves

**6. Frame and Mounting:**
- 3-point hitch
- Boom height adjustment
- Stability mechanisms

---

#### Boom Specifications

**Boom Height:**
$$
h = \frac{W}{2 \times \tan(\theta/2)}
$$

Where:
- h = Height above target (cm)
- W = Nozzle spacing (cm)
- Œ∏ = Spray angle (degrees)

**For 110¬∞ nozzle and 50 cm spacing:**
$$
h = \frac{50}{2 \times \tan(55¬∞)} = \frac{50}{2 \times 1.428} = 17.5 \text{ cm}
$$

**Typical boom height:** 40-60 cm above crop canopy

---

#### Nozzle Overlap

For uniform coverage, adjacent spray patterns must overlap by 100% at the target height.

**Overlap calculation:**
$$
\text{Overlap (\%)} = \left(\frac{2S - W}{2S}\right) \times 100
$$

Where:
- S = Spray swath width from one nozzle
- W = Nozzle spacing

**For proper coverage:** Overlap should be approximately 100%

$$

Where:

### Nozzle Discharge

**Theoretical Discharge:**
$$
Q = A \times V
$$

Where:
- Q = Discharge (L/min or cc/min)
- A = Orifice area (cm¬≤)
- V = Velocity (m/s)

**Velocity from pressure:**
$$
V = \sqrt{2gH} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times H}
$$

Where H is pressure head in meters.

**For pressure in bars:**
$$
H (m) = \frac{P (\text{bar}) \times 10}{1} = 10 \times P
$$

**Simplified discharge formula:**
$$
Q = C_d \times A \times \sqrt{2gH}
$$

Where C_d = Discharge coefficient (0.65-0.85)

---

### Practical Nozzle Discharge Formula

$$
Q = K \sqrt{P}
$$

Where:
- Q = Discharge (L/min)
- K = Nozzle constant (depends on orifice size)
- P = Pressure (bar)

**Example:** A nozzle with K = 0.8 at 4 bar
$$
Q = 0.8 \times \sqrt{4} = 0.8 \times 2 = 1.6 \text{ L/min}
$$

## ‚öôÔ∏è Pumps

### Types of Pumps

#### 1. Piston Pump

**Design:**
- Reciprocating piston in cylinder
- Inlet and outlet valves
- Crankshaft mechanism

**Working:**
- **Suction stroke:** Piston moves back, inlet opens, liquid enters
- **Delivery stroke:** Piston moves forward, outlet opens, liquid pressurized

**Features:**
- High pressure: 10-30 bar
- Pulsating flow
- Requires pressure chamber for smooth spray
- Self-priming

**Uses:**
- Power sprayers
- High-pressure applications

**Capacity:** 10-100 L/min

---

#### 2. Diaphragm Pump

**Design:**
- Flexible rubber/neoprene diaphragm
- Reciprocating motion
- Inlet and outlet valves

**Working:**
- **Suction:** Diaphragm moves back, creates vacuum, liquid enters
- **Delivery:** Diaphragm pushed forward, pressurizes liquid

**Features:**
- Medium pressure: 5-15 bar
- Less wear (no metal-to-liquid contact)
- Chemical resistant
- Self-priming

**Uses:**
- Knapsack sprayers
- Power sprayers

**Capacity:** 5-50 L/min

---

#### 3. Centrifugal Pump

**Design:**
- Rotating impeller in casing
- Centrifugal force principle
- No valves

**Working:**
- Impeller rotates at high speed
- Liquid enters at center
- Centrifugal force throws outward
- Pressure developed

**Features:**
- Low to medium pressure: 2-8 bar
- Smooth, continuous flow
- Simple construction
- Not self-priming
- High capacity

**Uses:**
- Boom sprayers
- High-capacity applications

**Capacity:** 50-500 L/min

---

#### 4. Roller/Roller Vane Pump

**Design:**
- Rotating roller in eccentric housing
- Rollers slide in and out
- Creates expanding/contracting chambers

**Working:**
- Rollers trap liquid in chambers
- Volume changes create suction and pressure
- Continuous rotation

**Features:**
- Medium pressure: 5-10 bar
- Smooth flow
- Simple
- Self-priming

**Uses:**
- Boom sprayers

**Capacity:** 20-100 L/min

---

### Pump Comparison

| Pump Type | Pressure | Flow | Self-priming | Smoothness | Cost |
|-----------|----------|------|--------------|------------|------|
| **Piston** | High (20-30 bar) | Medium | Yes | Pulsating | High |

## üìè Sprayer Calibration

### Why Calibration?

**Importance:**
- Ensures correct chemical dosage
- Prevents under-application (ineffective)
- Prevents over-application (wastage, toxicity)
- Uniform coverage
- Safety compliance

**When to calibrate:**
- Before each season
- When changing chemicals
- When nozzles are worn
- After repairs

---

### Calibration Methods

#### Method 1: Area-Based Calibration

**Steps:**

**Step 1:** Mark a test area
- Usually 100 m¬≤ (10 m √ó 10 m)

**Step 2:** Fill sprayer with known volume of water
- Example: 10 liters

**Step 3:** Spray the test area at normal speed
- Walk at constant pace
- Use normal spraying technique

**Step 4:** Measure remaining water
- Example: 2 liters left
- Water used = 10 - 2 = 8 liters

**Step 5:** Calculate application rate per hectare

$$
\text{Application rate (L/ha)} = \frac{\text{Volume used (L)}}{\text{Area (m}^2\text{)}} \times 10000
$$

**Example:**
$$
\text{Rate} = \frac{8}{100} \times 10000 = 800 \text{ L/ha}
$$

**Step 6:** Check against recommendation
- If recommended: 600 L/ha
- Current: 800 L/ha ‚Üí Too much

**Step 7:** Adjust
- Walk faster, or
- Close nozzle partially, or
- Reduce pressure

---

#### Method 2: Stationary Output Test

**Steps:**

**Step 1:** Collect nozzle output for fixed time
- Place container under nozzle
- Spray for 1 minute at operating pressure
- Measure volume collected

**Step 2:** Calculate actual output

Example: 1.2 L collected in 1 minute = 1.2 L/min

**Step 3:** Calculate theoretical requirement

**Formula:**
$$
q = \frac{Q \times W \times S}{600}
$$

Where:
- q = Required nozzle output (L/min)
- Q = Desired application rate (L/ha)
- W = Nozzle spacing (m)
- S = Speed (km/h)

**Example:**
- Q = 600 L/ha
- W = 0.5 m (50 cm spacing)
- S = 6 km/h

$$
q = \frac{600 \times 0.5 \times 6}{600} = \frac{1800}{600} = 3 \text{ L/min}
$$

Wait, this is total for boom. For one nozzle on boom with width 6m:
Number of nozzles = 6/0.5 = 12

Per nozzle = 3/12 = 0.25 L/min

**Step 4:** Compare and adjust
- Actual: 1.2 L/min (for handheld)
- Compare with requirement

---

#### Method 3: For Boom Sprayers

**Steps:**

**Step 1:** Measure actual travel speed
- Mark 50 m distance
- Time how long to cover at normal speed
- Calculate speed (m/s or km/h)

**Example:**
50 m in 30 seconds = 50/30 = 1.67 m/s = 6 km/h

**Step 2:** Collect output from all nozzles
- Spray into containers for 1 minute
- Measure each nozzle output
- Check uniformity (variation should be <10%)

**Step 3:** Calculate application rate

$$
\text{Application rate (L/ha)} = \frac{Q \times 600}{W \times S}
$$

Where:
- Q = Total flow rate (L/min)
- W = Boom width (m)
- S = Speed (km/h)

**Example:**
- Total flow = 24 L/min (12 nozzles √ó 2 L/min each)
- Boom width = 6 m
- Speed = 6 km/h

$$
\text{Rate} = \frac{24 \times 600}{6 \times 6} = \frac{14400}{36} = 400 \text{ L/ha}
$$

**Step 4:** Adjust if needed
- Change nozzles (different size)
- Adjust pressure
- Change speed

---

### Calibration Formula Summary

**General application rate:**
$$
\text{Application rate (L/ha)} = \frac{Q \times 600}{W \times S}
$$

Where:
- Q = Flow rate (L/min)
- W = Swath width (m)
- S = Speed (km/h)

**For hand sprayers:**
$$
\text{Application rate} = \frac{\text{Volume used}}{\text{Area covered}} \times 10000
$$

## üìä Performance Parameters

### 1. Application Rate

**Definition:** Volume of spray applied per unit area

**Unit:** L/ha (liters per hectare)

**Categories:**
- High volume: >500 L/ha
- Medium volume: 50-500 L/ha
- Low volume: 5-50 L/ha
- Ultra-low volume: <5 L/ha

**Factors affecting:**
- Nozzle size
- Pressure
- Travel speed
- Swath width

---

### 2. Swath Width

**Definition:** Width of area covered in one pass

**For handheld:** 1-3 m (operator controls)

**For boom:** Fixed by boom length (6-24 m)

**Formula for field capacity:**
$$
\text{Field capacity (ha/h)} = \frac{W \times S}{10}
$$

---

### 3. Uniformity Coefficient

**Definition:** Measure of how uniform the application is

**Formula:**
$$
\text{UC} = 100 \times \left(1 - \frac{\sum |x_i - \bar{x}|}{2n\bar{x}}\right)
$$

Where:
- x_i = Individual nozzle output
- xÃÑ = Mean output
- n = Number of nozzles

**Or simplified variation:**
$$
\text{Variation (\%)} = \frac{\text{Std deviation}}{\text{Mean}} \times 100
$$

**Acceptable:** Variation <10%

---

### 4. Droplet Size Distribution

**Measured by:** Laser diffraction or image analysis

**Parameters:**

**Volume Median Diameter (VMD or Dv50):**
- 50% of spray volume in droplets smaller than this
- Key indicator

**Dv10:** 10% volume in droplets smaller
**Dv90:** 90% volume in droplets smaller

**Spread Factor:**
$$
\text{Spread} = \frac{\text{Dv90}}{\text{Dv10}}
$$

Lower spread = more uniform size

---

### 5. Spray Angle

**Definition:** Angle of spray cone from nozzle

**Measurement:** At specified distance from nozzle

**Common angles:**
- Hollow cone: 60-80¬∞
- Solid cone: 60-90¬∞
- Flat fan: 80-110¬∞
- Flood jet: 100-140¬∞

**Affects:**
- Coverage width
- Boom height
- Overlap requirement

---

### 6. Drift

**Definition:** Movement of spray droplets away from target

**Causes:**
- Small droplets (<100 microns)
- Wind
- High pressure
- Boom height

**Measurement:**
- Collectors at distances from spray path
- Fluorescent tracers
- Percentage of applied chemical

**Reduction methods:**
- Use coarser droplets
- Reduce boom height
- Use drift-reducing nozzles
- Apply in calm weather
- Use drift-control additives

---

## ‚ö†Ô∏è Drift and Safety

### Drift Factors

**1. Droplet Size (MOST IMPORTANT):**
$$
\text{Terminal velocity} = \frac{d^2 (\rho_p - \rho_a) g}{18 \mu}
$$

Where:
- d = Droplet diameter
- œÅ_p = Droplet density
- œÅ_a = Air density
- Œº = Air viscosity

**Key insight:** Smaller droplets fall much slower ‚Üí more drift

| Droplet Size | Terminal Velocity | Drift Potential |
|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 20 microns | 0.012 m/s | Extreme |
| 50 microns | 0.08 m/s | High |
| 100 microns | 0.3 m/s | Medium |
| 200 microns | 1.2 m/s | Low |
| 400 microns | 4.8 m/s | Very low |

---

**2. Wind Speed:**
- <5 km/h: Calm (good for spraying)
- 5-10 km/h: Light (acceptable)
- 10-15 km/h: Moderate (caution)
- >15 km/h: Strong (avoid spraying)

**3. Boom Height:**
- Lower height = less drift
- Maintain minimum for overlap
- 40-60 cm recommended

**4. Pressure:**
- Higher pressure = smaller droplets
- Use lowest effective pressure

**5. Temperature and Humidity:**
- Hot, dry conditions increase evaporation
- Small droplets evaporate before reaching target

---

### Drift Reduction Techniques

**1. Nozzle Selection:**
- Use air-induction nozzles
- Pre-orifice designs
- Larger orifice sizes

**2. Additives:**
- Drift-control agents
- Increase droplet size
- Reduce fine droplets

**3. Operational:**
- Spray in early morning/evening
- Avoid windy conditions
- Maintain buffer zones

**4. Equipment:**
- Boom height control
- Pressure regulation
- Shielded booms

---

### Safety Measures

**Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):**
- ‚úì Protective clothing (coveralls)
- ‚úì Gloves (chemical resistant)
- ‚úì Boots
- ‚úì Face shield or goggles
- ‚úì Respirator (for toxic chemicals)
- ‚úì Hat

**During Operation:**

- Don't eat/drink/smoke while spraying

**After Operation:**

- Clean protective gear
- Dispose of containers properly

- Store chemicals safely

**Emergency:**
- Have first aid kit
- Know emergency contacts
- Follow chemical-specific antidotes

## üß™ Chemical Mixing and Concentration

### Concentration Calculations

**From percentage to amount:**
$$
\text{Amount of chemical (L or kg)} = \frac{\text{Required \% } \times \text{ Total volume}}{100}
$$

**Example:** 2% solution in 100 L
$$
\text{Chemical} = \frac{2 \times 100}{100} = 2 \text{ L (or kg)}
$$
Water = 100 - 2 = 98 L

---

**From recommended dose to dilution:**

**Given:** 2 L/ha of concentrated chemical, Apply 600 L/ha spray

**Dilution in tank:**
$$
\text{Concentration in tank (\%)} = \frac{2}{600} \times 100 = 0.33\%
$$

**For 100 L tank:**
$$
\text{Chemical needed} = \frac{0.33 \times 100}{100} = 0.33 \text{ L} = 330 \text{ ml}
$$

---

### Mixing Order

**Correct sequence:**

1. **Fill tank half with water**
2. **Add wettable powders (WP)** - with agitation
3. **Add water-dispersible granules (WG)**
4. **Add flowables (F)**
5. **Add emulsifiable concentrates (EC)**
6. **Add soluble liquids (SL)**
7. **Add adjuvants/surfactants**
8. **Fill tank to full volume**
9. **Agitate thoroughly**

**Never:**
- Add chemicals to empty tank
- Mix concentrates directly
- Mix incompatible chemicals

**Weekly:**
- Inspect hoses for cracks
- Check pump for leaks
- Test pressure relief valve
- Lubricate moving parts

- Calibrate sprayer
- Check nozzle wear (replace if flow >10% higher)
- Inspect pump valves
- Clean tank thoroughly

**Seasonal:**
- Complete overhaul
- Replace worn parts
- Check pump pressure capacity
- Store properly during off-season

### Nozzle Wear

**Signs of wear:**
- Flow rate increased by >10%
- Distorted spray pattern
- Uneven distribution

**Testing:**
- Collect output for 1 minute
- Compare with new nozzle
- Replace if >10% difference

**Life:** 200-500 hours (depending on chemical)

## üìà Important Formulas Summary

### Application Rate
$$
\text{Application rate (L/ha)} = \frac{Q \times 600}{W \times S}
$$

### Nozzle Discharge
$$
Q = K\sqrt{P}
$$

### Field Capacity
$$
\text{FC (ha/h)} = \frac{W \times S}{10}
$$

### Boom Height
$$
h = \frac{W}{2 \tan(\theta/2)}
$$

### Calibration (Area Method)
$$
\text{Rate (L/ha)} = \frac{\text{Volume used}}{\text{Area (m}^2\text{)}} \times 10000
$$

### Concentration
$$
\text{Chemical amount} = \frac{\text{Required \% } \times \text{ Total volume}}{100}
$$
